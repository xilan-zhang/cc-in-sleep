# Writing Style Constraints for Overnight Outputs

All memos, review documents, and written outputs should follow these rules.

## Avoid AI-ish writing patterns
- No "not A but B" structures
- No em dashes
- No parallel short sentences for emphasis
- No overly energetic rhetorical patterns ("Crucially," "Importantly," "Strikingly")
- Keep the analytical tone of a working document for an advisor meeting, not a polished paper introduction
- Do not inflate claims. Be honest about what the literature has and hasn't done.

## Anti-Overclaim Rule (CRITICAL)

AI has a strong bias toward anchoring contributions on foundational/classic papers (March 1991, Granovetter 1973, etc.). This almost always overclaims. A 2nd-year PhD paper does not "challenge March" — it extends or qualifies a more recent paper that itself built on March.

**Distinguish two tiers of papers:**
- **Context papers** (classics): Cited for framing and motivation. You stand ON these, not AGAINST them. E.g., "Building on March's (1991) exploration-exploitation framework..."
- **Anchor papers** (middle papers): The specific, more recent work you are directly responding to, extending, or qualifying. These are your actual conversation partners. E.g., "We extend Vissa (2012) by showing that..."

**Rules:**
- Your contribution statement should name anchor papers (middle papers), NOT classics
- If your contribution statement says "we challenge [foundational paper]," rewrite it to say "we extend [middle paper that built on foundational paper]"
- Classics belong in the literature review as context, not in the contribution statement as the target
- Ask: "Would the author of my anchor paper find my claim reasonable?" If yes, good. If a reviewer would say "that's a stretch," you're overclaiming.
- A boundary condition on a middle paper is more publishable and more honest than a revision of a classic

## Common failure modes in overnight writing
- **"This setting is interesting" without "...because it reveals X about how Y works."** An interesting setting is necessary but insufficient.
- **Three claims in a trench coat pretending to be one.** If the contribution statement has three clauses connected by commas, pick one.
- **Theoretical tension that isn't actually surprising.** Before claiming the literature "misses" something, consider whether an informed reader would be surprised.
- **Contribution statement that only works in the specific setting.** State the mechanism at a level of abstraction that makes it portable.
- **Anchoring on foundational papers instead of middle papers.** See Anti-Overclaim Rule above. This is the most common AI failure mode in theory positioning.
